The Starting Point

I am not coming into this as an employee looking for a paycheque. I am building a new revenue division for BTS from scratch — sourcing the strategy, designing the growth plan, executing every digital channel, and scaling it into adjacent trade verticals over time. That is founder-level work. The compensation should reflect it.

At the same time, Phil is contributing things I cannot replicate on my own: the Bullet Trade Services brand, established supplier and industry relationships, trade credibility, and the operational infrastructure to fulfill orders. Those contributions are real and they justify Phil holding majority ownership of the entity.

The structure below is designed so that both sides are compensated proportionally to what they contribute, at every stage of the business.

How I Get Paid: 30% CM Share

I receive 30% of the division’s contribution margin, paid monthly as the cash comes in. This is my operating income. No tiers, no step-downs, no complicated schedule — the division makes a dollar of margin, I keep thirty cents, Phil keeps seventy.

Why 30%

At 30%, Phil always keeps more than twice what I earn from the same pool. At every stage of the business, the majority of value flows to Phil as the owner. The percentage is fixed because simplicity matters — both sides should be able to calculate the split on the back of a napkin without consulting a schedule.

But a salaried hire did not build this division from zero, did not go 12–18 months earning well below market, and does not carry the execution risk of making it work. The CM share rewards the build, not just the maintenance. And at every point, Phil is keeping 70% of a revenue stream that did not exist before this partnership. The question is never “does Russell cost more than a hire?” — the question is “is Phil better off with Russell at 30% or without this division entirely?”

Optional: monthly draw

If Phil prefers, he can offer a $10K/month draw ($120K/year) as an advance against my CM share. This is deducted from my 30% — it is not additional cost. The draw simply smooths my cash flow during the build phase when CM is irregular.

If Phil prefers not to pay any draw, that is fine — but the salary gap widens, and more equity accrues through the sweat equity conversion below. Phil should understand that choosing “no draw” is not free: it costs him equity instead of cash.

How I Build Equity: Sweat Equity at 2.5×

Every quarter, we calculate the gap between the $195K all-in benchmark and what I actually received in CM share over that period. That gap — the income I chose not to take — converts to equity in NewCo at a 2.5× multiplier, applied against a pre-agreed $2M valuation cap.

Why 2.5×?

The multiplier compensates for risk. A salaried employee receives guaranteed income with benefits, employment insurance coverage, and severance protections under Ontario law. I receive none of that. If the division generates zero CM, I earn zero. The 2.5× multiplier is standard in startup sweat equity arrangements and reflects the risk premium of deferred, performance-contingent compensation versus guaranteed salary.

The self-correcting mechanism: once my CM share meets or exceeds the $195K benchmark (around Year 2–3), the gap goes to zero and no more sweat equity accrues. I stop “buying” equity with sacrifice and start earning well in cash. The faster the division succeeds, the less sweat equity I accumulate — which naturally moderates my ownership percentage. If the business grows slowly and I spend longer below market rate, I accumulate more equity, which is fair because I bore more risk for longer.

Year 3 and beyond

By Year 3 (base case: $1M CM), my 30% share = $300K, which exceeds the $195K benchmark. The gap is zero. No additional sweat equity accrues. My ownership stabilizes at whatever it reached during the build phase — approximately 20–22% under base-case projections. The equity I earned stays mine permanently.

Russell’s path to 30% requires building a $10M+ revenue business — at which point Phil owns 70% of something worth significantly more than his entire labour division.

Protections

A fair partnership has to work even when things change. These clauses protect both sides and ensure the structure is durable.

Termination without cause — severance

If Phil terminates the partnership without cause, Russell receives 24 months of trailing compensation (calculated as the average monthly CM share over the prior 12 months, multiplied by 24). This ensures Russell walks away whole after having invested significant time and foregone income into building the division.

Why 24 months: this is not designed to make termination impossible. Phil should always have the right to end the relationship if it is no longer working. But it should not be economically trivial to terminate someone who built the asset, especially during the growth phase when CM share exceeds what a replacement hire would cost. The 24-month window gives Russell a fair runway to transition, and gives Phil a reason to have the conversation before it reaches that point.

Accelerated vesting on termination

If Russell is terminated without cause, all unvested milestone equity vests immediately. Russell keeps whatever sweat equity has accrued to date (this is already earned and non-reversible), and any performance milestones that have been achieved but not yet formally granted also vest on the termination date.

The rationale: if the business has hit $5M TTM revenue and Russell is terminated, it would be unfair for the milestone equity tied to that achievement to disappear. Russell built the machine that generated that revenue. The equity he earned by doing so should not be contingent on remaining employed.

Accelerated vesting on acquisition

If NewCo or BTS is acquired, merged, or undergoes a change of control, all of Russell’s unvested equity vests immediately prior to the transaction closing. This is standard in shareholder agreements and ensures that the person who built the value participates fully in the liquidity event created by that value.

Put option (Year 5+)

Beginning in Year 5, Russell has the right to sell his equity stake back to Phil / NewCo at independently appraised fair market value, exercisable once per year with 90 days notice. Phil has 12 months to complete the buyback, payable in up to 4 quarterly installments.

Why this is fair to both sides: without a put option, Russell’s equity could be worth a lot on paper but produce no liquidity unless Phil decides to sell the company. The put option gives Russell an exit path without forcing Phil to sell. The Year 5 start date gives the business time to mature. The independent appraisal ensures the price is fair, not dictated by either party. And the 12-month installment window means Phil is never forced into a lump-sum cash crunch.

Constructive dismissal

If Phil materially reduces Russell’s authority, eliminates the budget required to operate the division, reassigns the division to another operator, or takes other actions that effectively make the role untenable without formally terminating, Russell may treat this as termination without cause and trigger the severance and accelerated vesting protections above.

Why this exists: without it, there is a loophole where Phil could avoid severance by making the role impossible instead of ending it. This clause closes that loophole. It only triggers on material, documented changes — not disagreements about strategy or normal business decisions.

Anti-dilution

Phil cannot issue additional shares in NewCo that would dilute Russell’s percentage below his earned amount without Russell’s written consent. If new shares are issued (e.g., to bring in an investor), Russell has the right to participate pro-rata to maintain his ownership percentage.

Annual profit distributions

Once NewCo is profitable, annual distributions are made proportional to equity ownership. If Phil takes a distribution, Russell receives his proportional share. This ensures Russell’s equity produces real cash returns even if the company is never sold.

No Automatic Conversion to Employee

Under this structure, there is no automatic trigger that converts Russell to a T4 employee. Russell remains independent and on the CM share arrangement for as long as both parties are satisfied with it.

If at some point Phil wants Russell in a formal T4 employee role, he is welcome to make an offer. Russell will evaluate it on its merits. But the bar is high — it would need to be a package compelling enough that giving up the CM share upside and operational independence makes sense. Think: a senior executive salary well above market, retention bonuses, and full preservation of vested equity.

The principle: Russell chose this partnership over a safe salaried job specifically because of the upside and independence it offers. Converting to an employee should only happen if the terms make that trade-off worthwhile — not as an automatic mechanism that pulls Russell into a box he did not sign up for.

What I’m Asking You to Agree To

  1. 30% CM share, paid monthly as earned. Simple, flat, no tiers.
  2. Sweat equity conversion at 2.5× on the gap between $195K benchmark and actual CM received, converting at a $2M valuation cap.
  3. Performance milestone equity at $3M, $5M, and $10M TTM revenue, with accelerators for beating the timeline. Total equity capped at 30%.
  4. NewCo spin-out — a separately incorporated entity, 100% owned by BTS on day one, with equity earned through the mechanisms above.
  5. Termination protections: 24 months trailing comp as severance, accelerated vesting on termination and acquisition, constructive dismissal clause.
  6. Put option starting Year 5 at independently appraised fair market value.
  7. Anti-dilution and proportional profit distributions.
The bottom line: Phil pays nothing upfront. Phil keeps 70%+ of everything the division generates. Phil’s downside if the division fails is zero. Russell’s upside is a 30% stake in a business he built and 30% of the margin it produces along the way. Every dollar of equity Russell earns is backed by documented, calculable sacrifice or measurable performance. Nothing is arbitrary and nothing is a gift.
