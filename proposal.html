The One-Paragraph Version

Bullet Trade Services has a real e-commerce opportunity — validated in a separate Market Assessment with sourced TAM of $25–70M/yr in Canada alone and an undersupplied, non-competitive category confirmed by external channel partners. What it needs is someone who will run the division as an operator, not a consultant. I'm proposing to do that in exchange for a tiered share of contribution margin (CM) that starts high during the unfunded build phase — because I'm taking no salary, no benefits, no severance reserve — and then steps down mechanically as CM grows, until at scale my total cash compensation is exactly what you would pay a T4 Head of E-Commerce and not a dollar more. Alongside, the division spins into its own NewCo sub-entity so costs are legible, firewalled from BTS, and the whole thing is independently sellable. Full terms are in the Compensation Memo; this page is the summary.

Compensation: Tiered Contribution-Margin Share

Pay is scheduled against CM bands. The first dollar of CM pays out at 40% (because there is no salary behind it); each subsequent band pays at a lower rate. This is mathematically self-limiting — the maximum cash at each band cap is knowable in advance:

$0 – $150K CM   @ 40%max $60K
$150K – $400K CM   @ 25%max $122K
$400K – $1.0M CM   @ 15%max $212K
$1.0M – $2.0M CM   @ 8%max $292K
$2.0M+ CM   @ 5% → converts to T4market-rate salary

This schedule by itself caps my cash comp at or below a senior director salary through the entire growth phase. At $2M+ CM, it mechanically converts to a market-rate T4 package — that is the ceiling built into the structure.

NewCo Spin-Out & Milestone Equity Vesting

The e-commerce division should be a separately incorporated entity — call it Bullet E-Commerce Inc. — wholly owned by Bullet Trade Services at formation. This cleanly legalizes the real costs Phil has flagged (a third of the warehouse becomes a market-rate sublease invoice, logistics hire becomes NewCo payroll, inventory float is on NewCo's own balance sheet). It also firewalls BTS from e-commerce liability and makes the division a sellable asset in its own right — worth materially more standalone than buried inside the operating business.

Inside NewCo, I earn a minority equity stake that vests on revenue milestones, not time. As equity vests, my CM share rate drops in lockstep:

  • 5% vested at $1.0M TTM revenue — CM share rate drops 25%
  • 10% vested at $2.5M TTM revenue — CM share rate drops 50%
  • 15% vested at $5.0M TTM revenue — CM share rate drops 75%
  • 20% vested at $10M TTM revenue or liquidity event — CM share goes to zero; I convert to a standard market-rate T4 salary + variable + benefits

At fully vested, Phil retains 80% of NewCo and I own 20% — both in a clean, sellable sub-entity. My cash compensation at that point is exactly what any business our size would pay its Head of E-Commerce. Not a premium.

Why NewCo instead of phantom equity against BTS: asking for equity in a 20-year-old operating business I had no part in building would not be fair. NewCo is cleaner. Phil owns 100% on day one, costs become legible invoices instead of hand-shake allocations, liability is firewalled, and the resulting entity is worth 2–3x more as a sellable asset than a division buried inside the operating company. This is the structure that protects both sides — it is governance, not just compensation.

Transition at Scale — How This Ends Cleanly

The automatic convergence

Between the tiered CM schedule and the NewCo vesting step-downs, my cash compensation mechanically lands at market rate by Year 4–5 without any renegotiation. No one has to fight about it. The schedule does the work.

If operational responsibility should transition

Once the CM share has dropped into the single-digit band, it is close enough to the variable component of a salaried role that either of these is clean:

  • Convert me to T4: market-rate base + variable + benefits as the formal Head of E-Commerce. Cash cost to NewCo = exactly the same as hiring from the outside.
  • Bring in a market-rate operator: I step back from day-to-day, retain only whatever NewCo equity has vested, and the division's operating cost stays at market rate. The structure never burdens the business with above-market comp.

Pre-trigger severance floor

Termination without cause before NewCo equity is fully vested = greater of 18 months trailing CM share or $150K. This prevents either side from weaponizing the schedule.

What happens if…

…the division doesn't work?

Minimal damage. If NewCo CM is zero, my comp is zero. Either party can exit with 30 days notice. Phil's cash exposure is bounded by inventory and the logistics hire.

…growth is slower than expected?

I earn proportionally less; Phil pays proportionally less. No fixed-cost drag. The structure absorbs downside automatically.

…growth is faster than expected?

The tiered schedule and NewCo vesting both step down; my cash share compresses toward a salaried cap. Phil is never stuck with above-market comp.

…Phil sells BTS?

NewCo is a separate entity with its own cap table. It can be sold with BTS, held back, or sold independently to a strategic buyer. My vested equity participates proportionally in NewCo's sale proceeds only.
