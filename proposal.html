Building a Scalable E-Commerce Division Together

Phil keeps 75%+ of the equity and 70–80% of the margin at every stage. Russell earns equity from documented sacrifice and cash from results. If it doesn’t work, Phil pays nothing.

The One-Paragraph Version

Bullet Trade Services has a real e-commerce opportunity — validated in a separate Market Assessment with sourced TAM of $35–55M/yr in core trades alone, expanding to $200–325M/yr across six industrial verticals using the same platform. What it needs is someone who will build and run the division as an operator, not a consultant. I’m proposing to do that in exchange for a tiered share of contribution margin (CM) that starts at 30% during the build phase — when I’m taking no salary, no benefits, no severance protection — and steps down to 20% and then 12% as the business scales. Alongside, the salary I forgo during the build converts to equity in a newly incorporated sub-entity (NewCo) at a 2.5× risk multiplier. Phil pays nothing upfront, keeps 75%+ of the equity, and keeps 70–80% of the CM at every stage. Full terms are in the Compensation Memo; this page is the summary.

Sweat Equity: Foregone Salary → Ownership at 2.5×

A senior hire for this role costs approximately $195K/year all-in (salary + CPP + EI + benefits + RRSP match + recruiting cost). Every quarter, the gap between that benchmark and what I actually receive in CM share converts to equity in NewCo at a 2.5× risk multiplier, applied against a $2M valuation cap.

Year 1 gap: $195K − $60K CM received= $135K × 2.5 = $337K equity credit
Year 2 gap: $195K − $150K CM received= $45K × 2.5 = $112K equity credit
Total credits ÷ $2M valuation cap= ~22.5% sweat equity

Once my CM share exceeds $195K (around Year 2–3), the gap goes to zero and no more sweat equity accrues. The faster the business succeeds, the less equity I accumulate. If it takes longer and I spend more time below market, I accumulate more — which is fair because I bore more risk.

Performance Milestones

Additional equity grants reward scaling beyond the initial trades:

  • $3M TTM revenue: +1% (accelerator: +2% if achieved in under 24 months)
  • $7M TTM revenue: +1% (accelerator: +2% if achieved in under 36 months)
  • $15M TTM revenue: +0.5% (accelerator: +1% if achieved in under 48 months)

Total equity from all sources (sweat + milestones) will range from approximately 20–25% depending on the growth trajectory. Phil retains 75%+ ownership and full operational control at all times.

NewCo Spin-Out

The e-commerce division should be a separately incorporated entity — Bullet E-Commerce Inc. — wholly owned by BTS at formation. This makes costs legible (warehouse sublease, logistics hire, and inventory float become real invoices on NewCo’s P&L), firewalls BTS from e-commerce liability, and makes the division a sellable asset in its own right — worth materially more standalone than buried inside the operating business.

Why NewCo instead of phantom equity against BTS: asking for equity in a 20-year-old operating business I had no part in building would not be fair. NewCo is cleaner. Phil owns 100% on day one. Costs become legible invoices. Liability is firewalled. And the resulting entity is worth 2–3× more as a sellable asset than a buried division. This is governance, not just compensation.

Protections — For Both Sides

Termination without cause

24 months trailing compensation as severance (average monthly CM share over prior 12 months × 24). All unvested milestone equity vests immediately. Sweat equity already accrued is non-reversible.

Accelerated vesting on acquisition

If NewCo or BTS is acquired or undergoes a change of control, all unvested equity vests immediately prior to the transaction closing. Both parties benefit proportionally from the liquidity event.

Constructive dismissal

Material reduction in authority, elimination of operating budget, or reassignment of the division to another operator triggers the same protections as termination without cause.

Put option (Year 5+)

Beginning in Year 5, Russell can sell his equity back at independently appraised fair market value, exercisable once per year. Phil has 12 months to complete the buyback in up to 4 quarterly installments.

Anti-dilution & profit distributions

No dilution of Russell’s equity without written consent. Pro-rata participation rights if new shares are issued. Annual profit distributions proportional to equity ownership once NewCo is profitable.

Why these protections exist: they cost Phil nothing as long as the partnership continues. They only activate if Phil ends the relationship or sells the company. Their purpose is to ensure that the person who built the division walks away whole if circumstances change — and that both parties share proportionally in any liquidity event.

No automatic conversion to employee

There is no trigger that forces Russell into a T4 role. Russell remains independent and on the CM share arrangement unless Phil makes an offer compelling enough to make the trade-off worthwhile. The partnership continues as-is for as long as both parties are satisfied.

What Happens If…

…the division doesn’t work?

Minimal damage. If NewCo CM is zero, Russell’s comp is zero. Either party can exit with 30 days notice. Phil’s cash exposure is bounded by inventory and the logistics hire.

…growth is slower than expected?

Russell earns less cash but accumulates more sweat equity (bigger gap = more equity credits). Phil pays proportionally less. The structure absorbs downside for both sides.

…growth is faster than expected?

Russell earns more cash but less equity (smaller gap). The tiered CM schedule moderates Russell’s share as CM grows. Phil’s effective take rises from 70% to 80%.

…Phil sells BTS or NewCo?

All unvested equity accelerates. Russell participates proportionally in the sale proceeds. NewCo is a separate entity with its own cap table, so it can be sold with BTS, held back, or sold independently.

  • No benefits, no EI, no employer CPP, no RRSP match

What Phil is risking

  • Logistics hire payroll ($60K–$80K/yr, paid by NewCo)

Phil’s cash exposure is bounded and every cost is legible on NewCo’s own P&L. Russell’s exposure is all labor and opportunity cost, and only pays out if CM actually happens.
